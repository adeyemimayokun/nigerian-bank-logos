# The awalogo story

## Why “awalogo”?

The name **awalogo** is a familiar Nigerian expression meaning “our logo.” It reflects the purpose at the heart of the project: building a shared, community-driven logo resource that everyone can use and help improve.

## It started with a recurring problem

The idea for awalogo emerged from its creator's experience working on digital products at [Sycamore](https://sycamore.ng).

Designers frequently needed logos for interfaces, presentations, and prototypes. Finding them was rarely difficult—they could search the web, download what they needed, and continue designing. But that approach did not translate well into development.

Developers needed dependable, optimized assets they could use without repeatedly downloading files, duplicating resources, or adding unnecessary weight to an application. Logos collected from random websites could also be outdated, poorly formatted, or inconsistent with an institution’s current identity.

What appeared to be a small design inconvenience was really a broader product-development problem.

## Validating the idea

Before building anything, the creator researched existing logo libraries to see whether the problem had already been solved.

Several useful platforms existed, but many had limited coverage of Nigerian institutions. Others contained outdated assets or appeared to have stopped expanding their catalogs, making it difficult to find logos for newer banks, fintechs, and financial products.

That gap revealed an opportunity for a more focused and dependable solution.

The project began with Nigeria’s financial ecosystem—commercial banks, microfinance banks, fintechs, payment providers, regulators, and other financial institutions. It was the environment where the problem had appeared most clearly, making it the right place to start.

## Bringing the idea to life

Building awalogo involved more than collecting images from the internet. The aim was to create a library people could trust.

The process began with a structured list of Nigerian financial institutions. Logo assets were then sourced from official websites, brand materials, and other institution-owned properties. Each asset was reviewed before being added, rather than automatically accepting any image found online.

The approved artwork was then prepared in practical formats—including SVG, PNG, and WebP—so it could support different design and development workflows. Raster logos are preserved in their original format rather than being inaccurately traced or redrawn as vectors.

The project also includes a searchable public catalog and an offline-first Figma plugin, both powered by the same structured logo library. Contribution and review workflows allow the collection to grow while maintaining clear sourcing and quality standards.

Today, awalogo contains **119 reviewed logos sourced from official properties**, with more institutions being researched and added over time.

## Built for the community

awalogo is an open-source project built on the belief that a resource called “our logo” should genuinely belong to the community around it.

The code and supporting tools are free to use and improve. The logos themselves remain trademarks of their respective owners, and their inclusion does not transfer ownership or imply endorsement.

This is only the beginning. The long-term vision is to expand beyond financial services into other Nigerian industries and, eventually, include institutions from across Africa and the rest of the world.

Designers looking for assets, developers building products, and contributors with verified corrections are all invited to use awalogo and help make it better.
